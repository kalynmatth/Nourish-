§ 02
Lighting
Shoot in natural light

Overhead daylight helps AI read labels, distinguish produce, and identify grains. Avoid harsh shadows across items.

Framing
Spread items out

Lay ingredients on a flat surface rather than photographing a crowded shelf. One clear flat-lay beats five cabinet shots.

Focus
Include packaging text

Canned goods, pasta boxes, and spice jars read best when labels face the camera. Angle items toward the lens.

What to include
Photograph expiry-risk items first

Wilting greens, softening produce, and open packages should be front and center — Nourish prioritizes what needs to be used soonest.

The Temperature Danger Zone

Bacteria multiply rapidly between 40°F – 140°F (4°C – 60°C). Keep cold food cold and hot food hot. Never share food that has spent extended time in this range.

The 2-Hour Rule

Cooked or perishable food left at room temperature for more than 2 hours should not be shared. In hot weather above 90°F, that window drops to 1 hour.

Assessing Produce

Surface blemishes and bruising are fine — that's what we're here for. Do not share produce with visible mold, strong fermented odor, or broken skin with interior discoloration.

Cooked & Prepared Food

Share same-day only. Food must have been stored covered and kept out of the danger zone. Label clearly: what it is, when it was made, and any allergens present.

Mold — Zero Tolerance

Visible mold means the entire item is unsafe — mold roots penetrate far beyond the surface. Do not cut off the moldy part and share the rest. This applies to bread, soft produce, and cooked food.

Packaging Integrity

Only share sealed packaged goods with intact packaging and a visible best-before date that has not passed. A dented or bulging can must not be shared.

Manifesto

Cook with conscience.

One-third of the food produced on this planet is thrown away while 733 million people go hungry. We built Nourish to stand in that contradiction.

Most recipe apps ask: what do you want to eat? Nourish asks: what do you already have? Snap your pantry. We see it. We write recipes that use the bruised tomato, the wilting herb, the half-onion in cling film.

Every recipe ships with a food security tip — practical wisdom on stretching staples, preserving leftovers, finding community resources, and shopping with intention. Cooking is an everyday political act.

The Recipe Board and Food Share tools on this platform exist for the same reason: to move food toward mouths, not landfills. No accounts. No social graph. No metrics. A bulletin board is enough.

"We do not need a kitchen of more. We need a kitchen of enough."

Waste is not inevitable. It is a design failure — in supply chains, in portion culture, in the assumption that abundance requires disposal. Nourish is a small correction.
